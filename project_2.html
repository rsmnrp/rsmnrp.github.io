Time Series Data Analysis Project

Introduction

In this project, my group and I aim to investigate the relationship between climate change and three variables (greenhouse gas emissions, mean sea level and anomalies in land-surface air and sea-surface water temperatures) by examining its patterns and trends.

The dataset used encompasses global, yearly records from 1880 to 2020. We have also conducted forecasts for these variables over the next 80 years, providing insights into the climate change issue in the foreseeable future.

Converting to time series

We convert our dataset, as well as all three individual variables into time series. We then used autoplot to visualise how each of these individual variables evolve over time.

Greenhouse gas emissions

There is an accelerating upward trend in greenhouse gas emissions, probably due to increasing human activities.

Temperature Anomalies

There is also an increasing upward trend in temperature anomalies. This can be attributed to the increase in greenhouse gas emissions above, further increasing the surface temperature of the Earth.

Mean Sea Level

There is also an upward trend in mean sea level for the past 140 years. This is probably due to thermal expansion and melting of land-based ice, causing the increase in Earth’s temperature.

Testing for stationarity

We used the KPSS test to evaluate if the mean and variance of a time series are constant over time. The null hypothesis suggests that the data is stationary. Hence, if our test statistic is more than 0.05, this indicates that our null hypothesis is rejected and thus requires differencing.

Since all of the above t-statistics exceed critical value at our chosen significance level of 5%, it suggests that data for the three variables are not stationary and we require differencing.

Differencing

We proceed to difference our data to remove trends and/or seasonality, making it stationary. Since our data focuses on macro-level phenomena and the influence of seasonal fluctuations might be negligible, we only use the ndiffs() function instead of nsdiffs()

Univariate time analysis

Greenhouse gas emissions

ACF/PACF

We use ACF/PACF to identify patterns and relationships between observations at different time lags. By examining the decay or cut-off patterns in the plots, we can determine the order of autoregressive (AR) and moving average (MA) components in models like ARIMA (Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average).

4th lag has significant spike before ACF values drop within the confidence interval, indicating the number of terms in the MA model, which is MA(4).

1 significant spike at lag 1 and PACF values fall sharply within the confidence interval after. This suggests an AR(1) model.
